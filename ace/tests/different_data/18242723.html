Compromised fronto-striatal functioning in HIV: An fMRI investigation of semantic event sequencing

Author links open overlay panel, , , ,
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bbr.2007.11.021Get rights and content

Abstract

The human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) damages fronto-striatal regions, and is associated with deficits in executive functioning. We recently developed a semantic event sequencing task based on the Picture Arrangement subtest of the Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale-III for use with functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) and found recruitment of dorsolateral prefrontal cortex and basal ganglia in healthy participants. To assess the impact of HIV on the functioning of the basal ganglia and prefrontal cortex, we administered this task to 11 HIV+ and 11 Control participants matched for age and education. Neuropsychological evaluation demonstrated that the HIV+ group had mild impairment in memory retrieval and motor functioning, but was not demented. Morphometric measurements suggested no atrophy in basal ganglia regions. The results of the fMRI analysis revealed hypoactivation of the left caudate, left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, and bilateral ventral prefrontal cortex in the HIV+ group. Functional connectivity analysis demonstrated less functional connectivity between the caudate and prefrontal cortex and basal ganglia regions in the HIV+ group. In contrast, the HIV+ group demonstrated increased activation of right postcentral/supramarginal gyrus, and greater connectivity between the caudate and this same anterior parietal region. The results of this study extend previous investigations by demonstrating compromised function of the caudate and connected prefrontal regions in HIV during cognition. This disruption of fronto-striatal circuitry likely precedes the development of cognitive impairment in HIV.

1. Introduction

The human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) is known to affect cognition, and the most pronounced declines from early to late stage HIV infection include changes in motor functioning, information processing speed, and executive functioning [39]. Within the domain of executive functioning, researchers have identified impairments in inhibition [18], [24], set shifting [41], manipulation within working memory [26], [41], and planning and cognitive sequencing [41], consistent with the hypothesis that changes in fronto-striatal circuitry may mediate HIV-associated cognitive decline. Structurally, neuroradiological studies have found that smaller caudate volume is associated with HIV disease [3], [4], [20], [45], and smaller caudate volume correlates with poorer performance on cognitive tests in patients with advanced HIV disease [21]. Volume loss in the medial frontal and premotor cortices is found in patients with AIDS, and thinning of the prefrontal cortex (PFC) in particular is associated with the severity of cognitive impairment in patients with AIDS [47]. Finally, functional MRI (fMRI) studies have found functional changes in PFC regions during tasks of attention and working memory [47], [9], [10], [14]. Together these studies provide evidence that HIV is associated with executive dysfunction, and with changes in fronto-striatal regions.

Although previous fMRI tasks used with HIV subjects have demonstrated impaired functioning in PFC [47], [9], [10], [14], and medial temporal systems [8], to date tasks that are known to activate the basal ganglia (BG) have not been used with this patient group. The purpose of the present study was to investigate the integrity of fronto-striatal circuitry in HIV during executive functioning, specifically by administering a semantic event sequencing task that is known to recruit both PFC and BG regions [48], [49]. The semantic event sequencing task administered in this study is an adaptation of the Picture Arrangement subtest of the WAIS-III [51]. This fMRI task requires subjects to plan ahead and sequence meaningful events chronologically, and reliably recruits PFC-BG circuits in healthy subjects [48]. This task has also been used to demonstrate changes in fronto-striatal functioning in patients with Parkinson's disease [49]. In the current study, between-group contrasts and functional connectivity analyses enabled us to examine how HIV affects BG-PFC regions. We hypothesized that the HIV group would demonstrate differential signal change in both the BG and PFC, and reduced functional connectivity between the BG and PFC, supporting the theory that compromise to fronto-striatal circuitry underlies cognitive deficits in HIV.

2. Material and methods

2.1. Participants

Eleven HIV+ (age = 40.8, S.D. 7.1, range: 29â€“52 years) and 11 HIVâˆ’ (age = 40.9, S.D. 8.7, range: 27â€“54 years) male Control volunteers participated in this study. Subjects underwent a neuropsychological battery in order to characterize the cognitive profile of our groups (see Table 1). To assess for group differences, we conducted independent samples t-tests on each of the neuropsychological tests, corrected for multiple comparisons (p < .005). To identify more subtle differences between groups, we also ran the analysis without correcting for multiple comparisons. To further examine cognitive performance, we created composite cognitive scores [17], [7] to assess the different domains tested, namely attention and executive functioning (EF), memory (MEM), and motor functioning (MOTOR). We also created a total cognitive score (OVERALL). To create these composites, we first converted each individual's raw score to a normative z-score based on published data [44]. For the EF score, we averaged the z-score across the WAIS-III Working Memory Index, Matrix Reasoning subtest, and Trails B. For the MEM score, we averaged the z-scores across the CVLT-II total learning, immediate recall, and delayed recall. For the MOTOR score, we averaged the z-scores from the Finger Tapping Test and Grooved Pegboard (both dominant and non-dominant hands). We averaged the z-scores from all of these tests to create the OVERALL score. We assessed for differences between groups based on these composite scores.

2.2. FMRI task

A picture sequencing (PS) and object discrimination Control (CON) task were administered to participants (Fig. 1) [48]. In the PS task, subjects saw four pictures in a scrambled order (i.e. plane taking off). A number appeared above the four pictures. Subjects were instructed to determine which of the four pictures was the nth in the sequence (i.e. the number 3 instructed the subject to find the 3rd picture in the sequence). In the CON task, subjects saw four unrelated pictures depicting objects of living things. Subjects were instructed to find the living thing. The Control task was designed to control for the visuospatial, semantic, and motor components of the PS task. In this condition, the letter â€œLâ€ appeared above the pictures. In both conditions, subjects were given 10 s to come to an answer. Following this, the word â€œGOâ€ appeared above the pictures for 2 s, instructing participants to make their response. Participants responded with their right hands by using a button-box with four buttons, one button corresponding to each of the spatial positions of the pictures. The task was presented using PsyScope [11]. The timing of the task was determined by behavioral piloting on a different group of HIV+ participants.

While in the scanner, subjects completed four block-design runs. Each run contained three PS and three CON blocks, and each block consisted of four trials (48 s/block). Subjects completed a total of 48 trials of each task type. Trials were randomized and counterbalanced across subjects using two task versions. A fixation period of 30 s preceded the first experimental block, and a fixation period of 40 s followed the last block of each run.

Prior to scanning, each subject was given a 35 trial self-paced practice session, followed by a 20 trial practice session with the timing constraints used in the scanner.

Behavioral data was analyzed using a Repeated Measures ANOVA with condition (PS vs. CON) as the repeated measure and HIV status as a between-subjects factor.

2.3. Scanning protocol

Scanning was performed on a Siemens 3 Tesla Trio scanner using a whole-head coil. One high resolution T1 weighted scan (MP-RAGE; matrix size 256 Ã— 256, thickness = 1.33 mm, TR = 2.5 s, TE = 3.3 ms, flip angle = 7Â°) was acquired for anatomical localization. Functional data were acquired along the AC-PC axis using T2* weighted gradient-echo, echo-planar pulse sequences (179 images per scan, TR = 2 s, TE = 25 ms, flip angle = 90Â°; slices = 28, voxel size = 3.125 mm Ã— 3.125 mm Ã— 5 mm).

2.4. Volumetric analysis

To assess basal ganglia atrophy, we calculated the volumes of basal ganglia structures (caudate, putamen, globus pallidus, nucleus accumbens) for each subject. Volumes were calculated from the T1 anatomical scan (MP-RAGE) using NVM software (semiautomated neuroanatomical measurement software available as open source at http://neuromorphometrics.org:8080). For each participant, we first ran an intensity-correction algorithm on their MP-RAGE scan to remove slowly changing variations in the MRI signal (bias fix software obtained from Neuromorphometrics). The T1 scan was then positioned along the AC-PC line. Next, we created outlines for each structure on each coronal slice containing that structure using isointensity contour drawing tools. All outlines were created by one experienced rater (R.M.), blinded to HIV status, following previous validated definitions [15]. Volumes were obtained by multiplying the total number of voxels within each ROI by the individual voxel volume (1.33 mm3). Independent samples two-tailed t-tests were used to compare the volumes of each of the eight ROIs between the HIV+ and Control groups.

2.5. fMRI data analysis

fMRI data were analyzed using SPM2 (Wellcome Department of Cognitive Neurology). Images were realigned to the first scan in the fMRI time series and unwarped to correct for residual movement-related variance [2]. All subjects had less than 4 mm translation and 2Â° head rotation prior to realignment. Images were next normalized to MNI305 stereotactic space (interpolating to 2 mm3 isotropic voxels; neurological convention), and smoothed using two different smoothing kernel sizes. A 4 mm3 Gaussian kernel was used to examine the within group activity in smaller structures such as the globus pallidus. An 8 mm3 Gaussian kernel was used for all between-group comparisons to account for differences in group variability. Statistical analyses were conducted using the general linear model. Design matrices were convolved with a canonical hemodynamic response function with time derivative. High-pass filtering with a cutoff period of 128 s was applied. For all fMRI data analyses, anatomical regions were determined using the Duvernoy Brain atlas [13]. Brodmann's areas (BA) within the DLPFC follow the specifications of Petrides and Pandya [34], and all other BA regions were determined using the MRIcro program [40].

2.5.1. Within group analysis

Contrast maps were created for each subject, and then used in a second-level analysis treating subjects as a random effect (one-sample t-test). Group averaged maps were corrected across the whole brain for multiple voxel-wise comparisons using the false discovery rate (FDR) procedure (p < .05). Threshold extent was 5 voxels. We assessed sequencing-related activity by comparing the PS blocks to the CON blocks (PS > CON). Because this was a block-design study, the analysis included both the 10 s cognitive component and the 2 s response period of each trial.

2.5.2. Between-group analysis

To assess between-group differences, a random effects model was used in which contrast maps of HIV+ and Control groups were compared using a two-sample t-test. Between-group differences were considered to be significant at p â‰¤ 0.001 (uncorrected) with an extent of 5 contiguous voxels. When examining between-group differences, we used a masking procedure that removed all voxels from the between-group contrast that were not shared by the masked (within group) contrast in order to ensure the direction of signal change observed in the between-group contrast [8], [49]. The within group masks were created at a threshold of p < .05 (see Section 3).

Our a priori regions of interest (ROIs) included the caudate, globus pallidus, dorsolateral PFC, and ventral PFC, hypothesized to support successful task performance [48], and parietal cortex, which has previously been shown to be hyperactive in HIV [9], [8], [10]. For these a priori ROIs, we first examined differences in the between-group contrast, and subsequently used the small volume correction tool in SPM2 to further assess signal change in ROIs that did not demonstrate differences in the between-group contrast. We centered 5 mm spheres on coordinates derived from an independent data set. Differences were considered significant at the voxel level using the family-wise error (FWE) procedure to correct for multiple comparisons. The independent data set consisted of the areas of difference between thirteen patients with Parkinson's disease (PD) and thirteen matched controls run on this same task [46]. Parkinson's disease is a neurological disorder that also impacts fronto-striatal structures, and consequently this data set provides an estimate of where we should expect to see fronto-striatal changes in HIV.

2.5.3. Functional connectivity analysis

Functional connectivity analysis was conducted between the caudate and the whole brain [19], [36]. A separate analysis was carried out for each hemisphere. For each subject, an anatomically defined caudate ROI was created using the WFU-Pick Atlas tool in SPM2 [25]. Signal intensity time courses from the most active cluster within the ROI during the PS condition (PS > fixation baseline) were extracted using the VOI tool in SPM2 (adjusted for effects of interest). These time points were then correlated with activation across the whole brain using a simple correlation procedure. Contrast maps for each subject were used in a second-level analysis for each group separately using a one-sample t-test. Maps were corrected across the whole brain using the family-wise error procedure (p < .05). Threshold extent was set to 5 voxels. In addition, we directly compared the results of the functional connectivity analysis between groups using a two-sample t-test [35]. Because we were interested in assessing differences in PFC-BG and parietal regions, we used a more liberal statistical threshold to examine group differences (p â‰¤ .005 uncorrected, with an extent of 5 voxels).

2.6. Associations between markers of HIV disease and fMRI signal

We conducted a post hoc analysis to determine if the markers of HIV disease were associated with fMRI signal. For each individual, we extracted the BOLD signal from a 5 mm sphere centered on the peak voxel showing the maximum between-group difference in the PS > CON contrast. We did this for both the left caudate and left DLPFC. For the HIV+ group, we correlated the activity in the PS condition in these two regions with CD4 count. For both groups, we correlated the activity in the PS condition with the composite neuropsychological scores.

3. Results

3.1. Neuropsychological battery

All results from the neuropsychological battery are reported in Table 1. When corrected for multiple comparisons, the only difference between groups was on the BDI-II, indicating that the HIV+ group reported more symptoms of depression. When we did not correct for multiple comparisons, the HIV+ group had poorer performance on the CVLT Delayed Recall (t = âˆ’2.1, p < .05), the Grooved Pegboard non-dominant hand number of drops (t = 2.4, p < .05), and Finger Tapping test (FTT) non-dominant hand (t = âˆ’2.7, p < .05). Although the HIV+ group had poorer performance relative to our Control group, the HIV+ mean for the CVLT Delayed Recall and FTT was within normal limits of age-matched normative data [44]. There were no significant differences between groups based on overall cognitive functioning as measured by the OVERALL z-score (t = 1.1, p > .2). There were also no differences between groups with respect to the EF (t = .15, p > .5), MEM (t = 1.4, p = .16), or MOTOR (t = 1.2, p > .2) scores. We also classified each participant's level of impairment using the OVERALL z-score, based on the criteria of Heaton et al. which suggests that a z-score <1 is indicative of mild impairment [17]. Using this criterion, none of our Control participants were impaired. One of our HIV+ participants had mild impairment (z = âˆ’1.1).

3.2. Volumetric analysis

To assess atrophy, we calculated the volumes of the basal ganglia structures. There were no differences between groups with respect to the volume of the left nucleus accumbens (mean, S.D. in mm3: HIV+ = 755, 144; Control = 787, 107), left caudate (HIV+ = 4045, 267; Control = 3916, 679), left globus pallidus (HIV+ = 1677, 118; Control = 1688, 268), left putamen (HIV+ = 5070, 500; Control = 5284, 758), right nucleus accumbens (HIV+ = 659, 102; Control = 653, 124), right caudate (HIV+ = 3976, 233; Control = 3902, 699), right globus pallidus (HIV+ = 1534, 122; Control = 1543, 244), or right putamen (HIV+ = 5089, 517, Control = 5281, 810; for all regions, t < .8, p > .4).

There were no differences in reaction time between groups (F(1,20) = 1.282, p > .2; mean RTs Â± S.D. in s.: PS task âˆ’ Control = .509 Â± .106, HIV+ = .450 Â± .129; CON task âˆ’ Control = .502 Â± .094, HIV+ = .450 Â± .129), and no main effect of condition on reaction time (F(1,20) = .217, p > .5).

3.3.2. FMRI results

3.3.2.1. Sequencing-related activations (PS > CON)

Both groups activated areas of PFC (including dorsolateral and ventrolateral regions), posterior parietal cortex, and occipital lobe, similar to that observed in young, healthy subjects (Fig. 2a) [48]. In addition, both groups activated regions of the basal ganglia, including bilateral caudate, globus pallidus internal segment, and the ventral mesencephalon (region including the substantia nigra). We also observed activation of the thalamus and posterior hippocampus.

When we directly compared the groups, the Control group showed increased BOLD signal change relative to the HIV+ group in frontal regions (Control > HIV+, PS > CON masked with the Control PS > CON contrast; Fig. 3.b.i; see Table 2 for coordinates and p-values). Small volume correction revealed reliable differences between groups in the left caudate (sphere centered at âˆ’4, 16. âˆ’2, z = 2.70, p(FWE) = .037; Fig. 3.a.i., Table 2).

We conducted a functional connectivity analysis between the left caudate and whole brain. The Control group showed correlation of left caudate activation with bilateral fronto-striatal regions, including caudate, putamen/globus pallidus, ventral mesencephalon (encompassing the substantia nigra; Fig. 3.a.ii., green ink), DLPFC, and ventral PFC (including VLPFC; Fig. 3.b.ii., green ink). The left caudate activation correlated with the left orbitofrontal cortex/insula and right orbitofrontal gyrus, but not with the DLPFC. The left caudate activation also showed correlation with bilateral postcentral/supramarginal gyrus.

We also examined connectivity between the right caudate and whole brain. We did not observe any functional connectivity between the right caudate and other BG, frontal, or parietal regions in the Control group, consistent with our previous findings that the left basal ganglia is more involved in the sequencing task [48]. In contrast, in the HIV+ group, we observed functional connectivity between the right caudate and bilateral caudate, anterior cingulate and multiple parietal regions, including bilateral postcentral/supramarginal gyri. We did not observe connectivity with the DLPFC, ventral PFC, or BG output nuclei. When the groups were compared, we observed greater connectivity in the HIV+ group relative to the Control group between the right caudate and postcentral/supramarginal gyri (HIV+ > Control; Table 3). Taken together, these findings from the connectivity analyses suggest that there is greater connectivity between basal ganglia and frontal regions in the Control group, and greater connectivity between the basal ganglia and anterior parietal regions in the HIV+ group.

3.4. Associations between markers of HIV disease and fMRI signal

In the HIV+ group, we observed a significant inverse correlation between CD4 count and fMRI activity in the left DLPFC (r = âˆ’.803, p < .01), demonstrating that higher CD4 count was associated with reduced activity of the DLPFC. There was no association between CD4 count and activity in the caudate (r = âˆ’.025, p > .5). For the cognitive composite scores, we observed a significant positive association between executive function and activity of the left caudate in the Control group (r = .614, p = .04), demonstrating that better performance on measures of attention and executive functioning was associated with greater activity in the caudate in the Control group. We did not observe this association in the HIV+ group (r = .220, p = .52; Fig. 5). There were no significant correlations between composite memory or motor scores and activity in the left DLPFC or caudate in either group.

4. Discussion

The purpose of our investigation was to examine the integrity of fronto-striatal circuitry underlying executive functioning in HIV. Our HIV+ and Control groups were matched for age and education, and results of the cognitive testing revealed only mild cognitive compromise, with subtle declines in long-term memory retrieval and motor functioning in the HIV+ group. There were no behavioral differences between groups on the fMRI task. Results of the structural MRI analysis found no evidence for atrophy of the basal ganglia in the HIV+ group. The results of our fMRI analysis demonstrated attenuation of activity within fronto-striatal regions in our HIV+ group. These results suggest that functional changes within the fronto-striatal system occur before structural changes, and may underlie later executive dysfunction in HIV.

Fronto-striatal circuitry is organized in multiple loops, in which the cortex projects to the input nuclei of the basal ganglia, then to the output nuclei of the BG, next to the thalamus, and finally back to the cortex [1]. The lateral prefrontal cortex projects to the basal ganglia input nuclei, with most projections targeting the caudate [1]. The BG output nuclei consists of both the globus pallidus internal segment (GPi) and substantia nigra (reticular part-SNr), and the SNr projects to areas within the ventrolateral PFC (VLPFC), while both the GPi and SNr project to the dorsolateral PFC (DLPFC) [29]. Within cortical areas, there are bidirectional connections between the DLPFC and VLPFC [34]. As such, the BG and PFC activity observed during the semantic event sequencing task reflects different areas of processing within fronto-striatal circuitry.

In our previous study examining this task in healthy participants [46], we proposed that the ventral PFC activity supported the encoding and retrieval of visual and semantic information as required by the PS task, given that the orbitofrontal cortex is connected to areas of the inferotemporal cortex that support visual object categorization and discrimination functions [5], and the VLPFC has been implicated in encoding and retrieval [6], [22], [27]. We also proposed that the DLPFC, a region hypothesized to support the monitoring and manipulation of information within working memory [33], was important in the reordering of the pictures. Finally, we proposed that the dynamic interplay between the BG and the PFC was necessary for successful sequencing on the PS task [48], as BG activity has been observed during tasks of sequencing ability [16], [23], [28], [31], [32], [37], [42], [43], [52].

In the present study, we observed compromise to fronto-striatal circuitry in the HIV+ group. First, when we directly compared the groups, we observed less activity within the ventral PFC in the HIV+ group. Likewise, we observed reduced connectivity between the left caudate and ventral PFC in the HIV+ group. Second, we observed less activity of left DLPFC, as well as attenuation of functional connectivity between the left caudate and DLPFC in the HIV+ group. Third, we observed hypoactivation of the left caudate in the HIV+ group. In addition, the between-group comparison of the left caudate functional connectivity analysis demonstrated reduced functional connectivity between the left caudate and basal ganglia output nuclei (globus pallidus) in the HIV+ group. We also found that in the Control group, caudate activity correlated with executive functioning as assessed by neuropsychological testing, whereas this association was not present in the HIV+ group. Taken as a whole, these results suggest that in HIV, a dysfunction exists within the BG and the PFC, and in the interactions between these regions. These functional changes are likely to underlie executive functioning impairments in patients with HIV.

Because structural changes within the BG have been reported in patients with HIV disease [3], [4], [21], [7], one possibility was that a reduction in BG brain tissue could explain the reductions in BG functional activity. However, there were no differences in BG volumes between our groups, and our findings suggest that there is a functional compromise of the BG prior to detectable volumetric change. One caveat is that a more recent investigation has documented that PFC thinning correlates with cognitive impairment in AIDS patients [47], and we did not assess PFC atrophy. Therefore, we cannot rule out the possibility that cortical thinning could have contributed to the hypoactivation of the PFC and connected BG regions in our HIV+ group.

In contrast to the hypoactivation of the frontal cortex, we observed hyperactivation of right postcentral/supramarginal gyrus (anterior parietal cortex) in the HIV+ group. In addition, we observed functional connectivity between both left and right caudate and bilateral anterior parietal cortex in the HIV+ group, but not in the Control group. These findings suggest that the HIV+ group required greater activation of anterior parietal regions and BG-anterior parietal circuitry to support sequencing. The right hemisphere in general and the right temporoparietal junction specifically have been hypothesized to be important in attention, particularly when directing attention towards behaviorally relevant stimuli not within current processing [12]. The anterior parietal activation observed in the HIV+ group was just anterior to the activation observed in the Control group, similar to the findings of other fMRI studies of HIV [9], [10], [8], and is consistent with the hypothesis that HIV disease induces a reorganization of the attention network that results in cognitive impairment if manageable load is exceeded [10], [50]. In addition, the results of the connectivity analysis suggest that this anterior parietal activity is correlated with caudate functioning in the HIV+ group. These results suggest that when primary damage to the fronto-striatal system occurs in HIV [4], [20], [21], [45], [47], increased activity in parietal attentional networks may reflect a compensatory mechanism.

A common limitation in clinical studies is medication regimes and treatment. In our study, ten of our HIV+ participants were on antiretroviral medications. Although the precise impact of these medications on BOLD signal is unknown, they are not known to affect the neurovasculature, and similar to other fMRI studies in HIV [10], it appears unlikely that a medication would induce a pattern of hypoactivation in one brain region (fronto-striatal), and hyperactivation in another (anterior parietal) simultaneously. A second finding that may be related to antiretroviral medication is our observed inverse correlation between CD4 count and DLPFC activity during the sequencing condition in the HIV+ group. Although this relationship appears paradoxical in that it indicates that HIV+ patients with better immune functioning had less DLPFC activity, it is consistent with previous work that found that higher CD4 counts correlated with basal ganglia structural changes [7]. One hypothesis is that while antiretroviral medications may improve immunological health, they may also have neurotoxic effects [30]. This hypothesis could be examined in future studies by examining, either longitudinally or across groups, the relationship between antiretroviral medication status, structural morphometry, and functional BOLD signal.

The high education status of our patients is also noteworthy. We carefully controlled for age, education, and estimated premorbid verbal IQ, suggesting that the differences in brain activity we observed are attributable to the HIV status of our patient group. The demographics of our study are consistent with previous publications using fMRI in HIV [8], [14], and suggests that changes to the functioning of the cortex, hippocampus, and caudate are all present even in highly educated HIV+ individuals with no impairing cognitive compromise. Future studies in HIV+ samples of average IQ will be important in replicating the generalizability of our findings to the HIV+ population as a whole.

5. Conclusions

In summary, we conclude that fronto-striatal functioning is compromised in patients with HIV. Our semantic event sequencing task is dependent on interactions between the prefrontal cortex and the basal ganglia [48], and this result adds to a growing body of literature suggesting that impairments to the fronto-striatal system underlie cognitive deficits in HIV. The HIV+ group exhibited this altered pattern of functional activity in the context of preserved behavioral performance, mild cognitive compromise, and intact subcortical volume integrity, suggesting that these functional changes occur prior to the structural atrophy and the severe cognitive changes that are associated with HIV-related dementia.
